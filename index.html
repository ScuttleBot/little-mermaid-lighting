Creating an Immersive Underwater World

Research compiled for St. Mary's High School production | February 2026

Overview

This research explores lighting design concepts for creating an immersive underwater atmosphere in a high school production of Disney's The Little Mermaid.

Key Goals

  • Create convincing underwater atmosphere through lighting
  • Use lighting to establish depth and distinct locations
  • Implement fog effects for water/land transitions
  • Focus on practical, achievable techniques for high school theatre

Water Caustic Effects - The Core Technique

The Professional Approach

Cinematographer Dion Beebe's technique for Disney's 2023 live-action film:

"My gaffer and I created water trays for theatrical moving lights that we could suspend above the sets, through which light could be projected. We then had our SFX team build a stirring apparatus to disturb the water and create the caustic lighting we wanted for sequences beneath the ocean surface."

Why this works: Creates the most organic, photorealistic underwater lighting effect possible.

High School Budget Alternatives

Option 1: Dedicated Water FX Lights (Easiest)

  • Chauvet Abyss USB - simulates multicolored water ripples
  • Works with or without fog
  • DMX controllable or standalone operation
  • Recommendation: Minimum 2 units to adequately fill stage
  • Cost: ~$400-500 per unit

Option 2: Moving Lights + Gobos (Most Versatile)

  • Use dual gobo wheels - morph between water patterns
  • Add motorized focus for depth variation
  • Combine with gobo rotation + prism for dynamic rippling
  • GAM Products has a recipe book specifically for water effects

Option 3: Budget Option - Source4 + TwinSpins

  • 4x Source Four ellipsoidals with TwinSpin attachments
  • Water ripple gobos (Rosco or GAM)
  • Slower but effective
  • Most affordable for schools with existing conventional inventory

Color Palette Strategy

From professional productions: Each location needs its own color identity

Ariel's Grotto / Ocean Surface

  • Colors: Light blues, turquoise, aquamarine
  • Mood: Bright, curious, hopeful
  • Technique: Higher key lighting with warmer accents on practical objects
  • Gels: Lee 200 (Double CT Blue), Rosco 60 (No Color Blue)

King Triton's Kingdom

  • Colors: Jewel tones - blues, purples, teals, emerald
  • Mood: Regal, majestic, vibrant
  • Inspiration: Real coral and anemones
  • Gels: Mix of Lee 724 (Ocean Blue), Lee 181 (Congo Blue), Lee 128 (Bright Pink) accents

Ursula's Lair

  • Colors: Dark purples, deep blues, black
  • Mood: Sinister, shadowy, threatening
  • Technique: Low key, high contrast, sharp shadows
  • Gels: Lee 197 (Alice Blue), Lee 181 (Congo Blue), Lee 126 (Mauve) for purple tones

Above Water Scenes

  • Colors: Warm whites, amber, golden tones
  • Mood: Realistic, sunlit, natural
  • Technique: Sharp focus, defined shadows, no ripple effects
  • Gels: Lee 204 (Full CT Orange) for sunset, CTO gels for warmth

Lighting Principles for Underwater Scenes

The Foundation: Backlight + Toplight Dominance

"Backlight and top light in blues and teal greens, then supplement with glowing warm tone fronts and sides to pull the actors out of the blue."
‚Äî Reddit r/lightingdesign community wisdom
  • Primary: Blue/teal from above creates "water overhead" feeling
  • Light filtering down effect mimics how light behaves underwater
  • Accent with warm tones (ambers, light pinks) from front/sides
  • Prevents actors being lost in blue wash
  • Creates essential depth separation

Critical Mistake to Avoid

"If your lighting isn't working, start turning things off."

From Disney California Adventure's Little Mermaid attraction lighting designer:

"I had a washed out, too bright, gray, ripple-less scene. I turned off approximately 90% of the dark blue wash, leaving just enough in shadowy areas to set the mood. I then turned off all of the ripple projectors [in problem areas]."

Lesson: Less is more. Over-saturation kills the effect.

The "Sunken Theater" Concept

From ABC's Little Mermaid Live! Production Designer Misty Buckley:

"We discussed the aesthetic and how we would immerse the audience in a theater that has been submerged underwater. The idea is that you'd stumbled into a beautiful opera house that was sunken 100 years ago... We used lots of effects in lighting and projection to make you feel like you're above water or below water."

Key Design Elements

  • Organic curved shapes - no hard edges (everything flows)
  • Vertical movement - aerialists, flown props create "floating" feeling
  • Layered effects - projection + LED + practical scenery + lighting
  • 360-degree environment - audience fully immersed
  • Hidden technical elements - magic without revealing the trick

Creating Constant Motion

"The ambition was to create a piece of work that always had movement in it, without that movement being reliant on a screen all the time."

  • Slow gobo rotation creates gentle water movement
  • Varying speeds between fixtures adds complexity
  • Nothing should feel static underwater

Fog for Water/Land Transitions

Creating the illusion of breaking the water's surface

The Technique

  • Dense low fog represents the water surface plane
  • Backlit fog creates ethereal, luminous boundary
  • Lighting shift: cool (underwater) to warm (above) as characters emerge
  • Fog dissipates upward = visual metaphor for rising through water

Practical Execution

  1. Position foggers at stage deck level
  2. Use fans to control direction and density
  3. Coordinate fog release with lighting cue (practice timing!)
  4. Transition should feel gradual (30-60 seconds), not instant
  5. Lighting shifts from cool wash to warm simultaneously

Pro Tip

Rehearse fog transitions extensively. Timing varies with venue humidity and HVAC. Have a crew member with stopwatch tracking dissipation rate during tech rehearsals.

Gobo Selection Guide

Essential Patterns for Underwater

Water Caustics / Ripples:

  • GAM water effect gobos (they publish a recipe book - get it!)
  • Rosco ripple patterns
  • Layer multiple gobos at different rotation speeds
  • Key: Vary the speed - not everything moves at same rate underwater

Texture & Depth:

  • Breakup patterns for rocky ocean floor
  • Organic shapes for coral/vegetation shadows
  • Slow rotation for gentle, natural movement

Equipment Options:

  • Best: Moving lights with dual gobo wheels + rotation
  • Budget: TwinSpins on Source4 ellipsoidals
  • DIY: Single gobo with manual rotation (labor intensive)

Additional Special Effects

Bubbles

  • Multiple bubble machines positioned above audience or at stage apron
  • Use during musical numbers, end of show, curtain call
  • "You can never have too many bubbles!"
  • Creates instant audience immersion

King Triton's Trident Magic

  • EL Wire (electroluminescent wire) for glowing trident effect
  • Battery-powered, flexible, bright
  • Cold to touch, water-resistant
  • Optional: Flash cotton + sparkle additive for "magic burst" effects

Projections

  • Professional media available (Matt Kizer Design - $600 rental)
  • DIY: Loop underwater footage videos
  • Project onto cyc, scrim, or shaped scenic elements
  • Combine with practical lighting for depth

Creating the Above/Below Water Distinction

Underwater Scenes

  • Cool color palette (blues, teals, cyans, purples)
  • Ripple/caustic effects ACTIVE
  • Softer edges, more diffusion
  • Moving patterns (slow, gentle)
  • Lower contrast, more atmospheric

Above Water Scenes

  • Warmer color temperature (CTOs, ambers, whites)
  • Cleaner, sharper light
  • Static (non-moving) effects
  • More realistic shadows
  • Higher contrast

The Transition

  1. Start transition 30-60 seconds before character fully emerges
  2. Release low fog to create "surface" layer
  3. Begin gradual fade: cool lighting DOWN, warm lighting UP
  4. Slow stop on ripple effects
  5. Fog dissipates upward naturally
  6. By the time actor is "above water," lighting is fully warm/natural

Key Takeaways

Making It Work for High School Theatre

  1. Prioritize water ripple effects - single most important element for underwater illusion
  2. Use color strategically - different locations = different palettes (don't make everything the same blue)
  3. Layer your effects - lighting + fog + projection when budget allows
  4. Don't over-blue - warm accents keep actors visible and prevent muddy, gray look
  5. Test fog timing extensively - transitions need rehearsal, vary by venue conditions
  6. Movement matters - even slow gobo rotation adds life to static scenes

Minimum Budget-Friendly Setup

  • 2x water effect lights (Chauvet Abyss or similar) OR
  • 4x Source4 + TwinSpins + water ripple gobos
  • 1x low fog machine
  • Selection of blue/teal/purple gels for conventional fixtures
  • Warm accent gels (CTOs, light pinks) for pulling actors out

Total estimated cost: $800-1500 if starting from scratch

Scene-by-Scene Lighting Notes

"Part of Your World" (Ariel's Grotto)

  • Challenge: Balance intimate moment with grand underwater setting
  • Lighter blues - surface water nearby
  • Warm practical light on collection of human objects
  • Build intensity as song builds emotionally

"Under the Sea"

  • High energy, vibrant color
  • Oranges and pinks per original film color palette
  • Keep movement constant (gobos, color shifts)
  • Brightest, most saturated scene in show

"Kiss the Girl"

  • Romantic, soft lighting
  • Above water - warm moonlight effect
  • Dappled light through "trees" (gobo breakup patterns)
  • Gentle movement from environment (fireflies, water reflections)

"Poor Unfortunate Souls"

  • Darkest, most theatrical lighting in show
  • Deep purples, dramatic shadows
  • Isolate Ursula with sharp special
  • Smoke/haze enhances light beams
  • Practical "magic" effects (contract glows, transformations)
